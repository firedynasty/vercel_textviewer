The southpaw advantage in boxing comes from the asymmetry and unfamiliarity it creates. Here's why it's often advantageous:
Unfamiliarity breeds difficulty. Since roughly 90% of boxers are orthodox (left foot forward, right hand power), most fighters spend the vast majority of their training and sparring against other orthodox fighters. When they face a southpaw, they're dealing with angles, timing, and patterns they rarely encounter.
The angle game changes completely. Orthodox fighters are used to circling away from their opponent's power hand (the right), but against a southpaw, that same direction now circles directly into the southpaw's power left hand. This fundamental footwork pattern that orthodox fighters have drilled thousands of times suddenly works against them.
Lead hand positioning creates problems. When orthodox meets southpaw, both fighters' lead hands (left for orthodox, right for southpaw) are on the same side. This creates an awkward dynamic where the orthodox fighter's jab - their most-used punch - is constantly being interfered with or blocked by the southpaw's lead hand position.
The power shots land differently. A southpaw's straight left hand comes from an angle that orthodox fighters see much less frequently. It can slip through defenses that work well against orthodox rights because the defensive patterns are less ingrained.
Muscle memory works against the orthodox fighter. Years of training create automatic responses, but these responses are calibrated for orthodox opponents. Against southpaws, these instinctive reactions often leave openings or put the orthodox fighter in poor position.
The advantage isn't absolute - skilled orthodox fighters can and do beat southpaws regularly - but the unfamiliarity factor gives southpaws a significant tactical edge, especially early in fights before opponents can adjust.

For a southpaw, it's actually easier to go left.

Southpaw stance = right foot forward, left foot back
When you step to your left, you're stepping with your back (left) foot, which gives you:
Better balance and control
Ability to maintain your stance structure
Can still throw punches while moving
Natural pivot direction
When you step to your right, you're stepping with your front (right) foot, which:
Can leave you off-balance temporarily
Harder to maintain stance integrity
More difficult to punch effectively while moving that direction
This is exactly what made Crawford so effective against Canelo. Crawford (southpaw) kept going to his left, which was:
His natural, easy direction to move
Canelo's weak side (putting Canelo in an awkward position)
So Crawford was moving in his strongest direction while simultaneously putting Canelo in his weakest position. It's like a double advantage - Crawford stays balanced and mobile while Canelo gets turned and off-balance.
For orthodox fighters, it's the opposite - they move more naturally to their right (stepping with their back right foot).
This is why the southpaw vs orthodox matchup creates such interesting tactical dynamics - each fighter's natural movement direction creates problems for the other, but the southpaw often has the advantage due to unfamiliarity.
