Great question! Fast piano playing — especially pieces like Mendelssohn's **Presto agitato** — comes down to a few fascinating things working together:

**Muscle memory over conscious thought** At high speeds, the fingers aren't being directed by conscious thought anymore. Through thousands of hours of practice, the motor patterns get "downloaded" into the cerebellum, so the hands essentially run on autopilot. Thinking about individual notes at tempo would actually *slow you down*.

**Finger independence training** Pianists spend years doing exercises (Hanon, Czerny, scales, arpeggios) specifically to make each finger operate independently. Most people's fingers naturally want to move together — pianists train that out of themselves.

**Relaxation, not force** Counterintuitively, tension is the enemy of speed. Fast playing requires deeply relaxed forearms and wrists. Tight muscles fight each other. The fastest pianists look almost effortless because they've learned to release tension between each keystroke.

**Chunking** Rather than reading note-by-note, expert pianists perceive patterns — a scale run, an arpeggio shape, a chord sequence — as single units. It's like how a fast reader sees whole words, not letters.

**The physics of the key** Piano keys only need about **50 grams of pressure** to depress. At speed, fingers are essentially just *dropping* onto keys with gravity, not pressing hard.

**Years of slow practice** The paradox: speed comes from practicing *slowly*. Drilling passages at half-tempo builds the neural pathways cleanly, so when tempo increases, the pattern is already perfect.

Yes! You've just discovered something that applies to basically **every precision sport** — the harder you *try*, the worse it gets.

**What's actually happening with your shot:**

When you rush or force it, you're introducing tension into your shooting arm, wrist, and fingers. Tense muscles are imprecise muscles — they fire unpredictably and fight each other, just like the pianist gripping too hard.

The slow, relaxed release lets your **wrist snap naturally** and your fingers guide the ball off cleanly at the end. That's where all your accuracy actually lives — the fingertip follow-through. Rush it and you bypass that entirely.

**The deeper thing going on** is that forcing a shot activates your *gross motor system* (big, powerful, imprecise), while a smooth release keeps you in your *fine motor system* (small, controlled, accurate). You literally switch into a different mode of movement.

**It also messes with your arc.** A rushed shot tends to be flat and line-drive because you're pushing rather than launching. The relaxed shot naturally produces a higher arc, which gives you a much bigger margin of error on the rim.

This is why players talk about **"letting the ball go"** rather than throwing it. Steph Curry is the perfect example — his release looks almost lazy, like the ball just floats off his fingers. That's not weakness, that's mastery of relaxation.

The pianist and the shooter are doing the exact same thing neurologically — getting their conscious, anxious brain *out of the way* and letting trained motor patterns do their job.
