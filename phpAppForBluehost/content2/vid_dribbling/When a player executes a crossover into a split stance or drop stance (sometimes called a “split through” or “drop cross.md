When a player executes a **crossover into a split stance or drop stance** (sometimes called a “split through” or “drop cross”), the **dribble ends at a very specific moment**: **as the player is dropping into the split or drop stance, the ball is picked up (caught) just as the body lowers into that athletic, wide-stance position**. This is not a “one-two stop” in the traditional sense (like a jump stop); instead, the player uses this split/drop stance as a **read-based tempo change**—often after a hard crossover or between-the-legs move—to **freeze the defender, evaluate the defense, and then explode forward or elevate for a shot**.

**In high-level breakdowns—**like those from elite trainer Tyler Relph—the dribble ends when the player is **in the low, wide stance with the ball protected**, just before deciding to either **attack off the dribble again or rise for a shot**. This is a foundational read-and-react move in modern guard play, especially against aggressive defenders.youtube

**Here’s the sequence, step by step:**

- **Crossover or advanced dribble move** to displace the defender.
- **Drop into a split stance** (knees bent, wide base, ball protected).
- **The dribble ends as you hit this posture**—**not** at a count of one or two, **but as you make your read**. If you decide to shoot, you pick up the ball immediately. If you decide to drive, you may take **one more dribble** to explode past the defender.
- **The one-two rhythm** is not about steps or dribbles, but about **decision-making**: you catch (pick up) the ball at “one,” read the defense, and at “two,” you either shoot or drive.

**Why this matters:**
 Placing the ball on the floor for **one more dribble before picking up** increases the time for the defense to react and contest your shot or drive. Elite players minimize unnecessary dribbles after the move, so the dribble **ends at the exact moment the player is in the split/drop stance**, ready to make their next play. This is fundamental for **efficiency and deception** in advanced shot creation.

**In summary:**
 The dribble ends **immediately as you hit your low, drop/split stance** after a crossover or hesitation move. This is the optimal control point for reading the defense and deciding whether to shoot, pass, or drive.
